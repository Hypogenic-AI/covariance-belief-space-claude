Human beliefs do not exist in isolation---they form structured, correlated systems.
Understanding the intrinsic dimensionality of this ``belief space'' has implications for
political science, survey design, and AI alignment, yet no prior work has systematically
measured it at scale. We generate \nbeliefs diverse belief statements and analyze their
covariance structure using three complementary methods: semantic embedding PCA over all
beliefs, persona response PCA (\npersonas synthetic personas rating \nbeliefsub beliefs),
and conditional belief influence PCA (\nanchors anchor beliefs $\times$ \ntestbeliefs test
beliefs). We find that belief space is dramatically lower-dimensional than random baselines:
only 9 principal components explain 50\% of response variance and 93 explain 90\%, compared
to 154 for random data. The dominant first component (28.6\% of variance) maps cleanly to a
progressive--conservative ideological axis. The conditional influence experiment reveals that
just 25 dimensions explain 90\% of how holding one belief predicts agreement with others.
These results suggest that the apparent diversity of human opinions can be largely captured
by a small number of latent ideological dimensions, and that approximately 25 well-chosen
diagnostic questions could reconstruct 90\% of an individual's belief profile.
